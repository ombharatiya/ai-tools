# AI tools and apps for a Lawyer

For attorneys who advise clients, research law, draft documents, and appear in court, bound by duties of competence, confidentiality, candor, and supervision. AI speeds up research and drafting, but it hallucinates cases, and courts have sanctioned lawyers who did not check.

> **Caution: this is a regulated field.** You must independently verify every citation, quotation, and proposition before filing or advising. Do not put client confidences into consumer or self-learning tools. Check each court's standing orders on AI use. The tools below assist, they do not discharge your professional duties.

## Who this is for

Attorneys in firms of any size, in-house counsel, and the paralegals and staff they supervise.

## Starter stack

With the caution that professional judgment and independent verification of every authority are required:

1. One purpose-built research platform your firm subscribes to: **Westlaw with CoCounsel** or **Lexis+ with Protégé**. Use the built-in citation validation (KeyCite, Shepard's) and still read the cases.
2. A contract tool matched to your work: **Spellbook** for small-firm drafting, or **Luminance** or **Kira** for larger review and due diligence.
3. A general assistant ([ChatGPT](../tools/ai-assistants/chatgpt.md), [Claude](../tools/ai-assistants/claude.md), or [Gemini](../tools/ai-assistants/gemini.md)) on a paid no-training tier, for non-confidential drafting and learning only.
4. **Clio** (or your firm's system) for practice management and intake.
5. A personal checklist: verify every cite, never paste client confidences into non-approved tools, disclose AI use where required.

## Cautions for this profession

- **Hallucinated citations are a sanctions risk.** Courts have repeatedly sanctioned lawyers for filing AI-fabricated cases. Even purpose-built tools hallucinate: a Stanford RegLab study found Lexis+ AI gave incorrect or misgrounded answers on more than 17% of queries and Westlaw's AI on roughly a third, and concluded vendor "hallucination-free" claims were overstated. Verify every citation and quotation before filing. This duty cannot be delegated.
- **Confidentiality and privilege.** Under ABA Formal Opinion 512, you must understand how a tool uses your input and protect client information, and informed client consent is generally required before entering confidential information into such a tool. Use tools that contractually do not train on your data, and a closed environment where appropriate.
- **Competence and candor.** Competence now includes understanding the technology's risks. You cannot present AI output you have not verified, and many courts require disclosure or certification of AI use. Check local rules for every court.
- **Supervision.** Supervising lawyers are responsible for associates' and staff's AI use. Have a written firm policy and review process.
- **Fees.** You generally cannot bill for hours the AI saved as if you spent them. Be transparent about how AI affects fees and costs.
- **Accuracy beyond citations.** Hallucinations also appear as plausible but wrong reasoning, misstated holdings, and outdated law. Treat AI output as an unverified draft from a junior person, not an authority.

## How to choose

If your firm provides Westlaw or Lexis, start there and lean on the citation validators, while still reading the authorities. Match the contract tool to your volume: Spellbook for solo and small-firm work, Luminance or Kira for heavy review. Keep general chatbots for non-confidential tasks on a no-training tier. The real differentiator is your verification discipline, not the tool.
